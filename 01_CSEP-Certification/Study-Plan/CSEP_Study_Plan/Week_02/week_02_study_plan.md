# CSEP Study Plan — Week 2: Life Cycle Concepts
**Certification:** INCOSE CSEP (Certified Systems Engineering Professional)
**Primary Reference:** INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook, 5th Edition (SEH5) — Chapter 2
**Exam Basis:** ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023 §6 — System Life Cycle Processes
**Week Theme:** How systems come into existence, evolve, and retire — and how SE processes relate to life cycle stages
**Target:** ~4 hours total study time across 7 days

---

## 🎯 Week 2 Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 2, you should be able to:

1. Define "system life cycle" using INCOSE SEH5 and ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 terminology
2. Name and describe all six ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 life cycle stages in order, including purpose and typical decision gate
3. Explain the difference between a **life cycle stage** and a **life cycle process** — a critical CSEP exam distinction
4. Describe at least three life cycle models (sequential, iterative, concurrent) and identify when each is most appropriate
5. Use the cost-of-change curve to justify early SE investment in the Concept and Development stages
6. Distinguish between a system life cycle, a project life cycle, and a product life cycle
7. Explain how the four ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 process groups relate to the six life cycle stages
8. Describe the purpose and outputs of a **decision gate** (stage transition review)
9. Identify the ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 clauses governing life cycle definition and stage descriptions
10. Apply life cycle thinking to explain why SE decisions made early have disproportionate impact on total cost

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| System Life Cycle | The evolution of a system over time, encompassing concept, development, production, utilisation, support, and retirement | SEH5 §2; ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Life Cycle Stage | A defined period within the system life cycle characterised by a particular type of activity and bounded by decision gates | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Life Cycle Model | A framework that identifies and describes the stages, decision gates, and overall approach used to manage a system's life cycle | SEH5 Ch2 |

### Day 2 — Tuesday | Life Cycle Stages — ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 §6.2
**Estimated Time:** 40 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Chapter 2 — the six life cycle stages in detail

#### Topics to Cover
- **Concept Stage** — identifying stakeholder needs, exploring concepts, refining to a preferred solution
- **Development Stage** — defining and realising the system, verifying and validating
- **Production Stage** — producing system instances, inspecting and testing
- **Utilisation Stage** — operating the system in its intended environment
- **Support Stage** — maintaining the system to sustain availability
- **Retirement Stage** — archiving, decommissioning, and disposing of the system
- Entry conditions and typical activities for each stage

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2, sections covering each life cycle stage
- [ ] For each stage, write: (a) its primary purpose, (b) one key input, (c) one key output
- [ ] Choose a real system you know (a car, mobile network, spacecraft) and map it to the six stages
- [ ] Review Day 2 Flashcards (Cards 9–20)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Concept Stage | The stage in which stakeholder needs are identified, concepts are explored, and a preferred solution is selected | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Development Stage | The stage in which system requirements are refined, the solution is designed, built, and verified; the system is validated against stakeholder needs | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Production Stage | The stage in which the system (or multiple instances) is produced, inspected, and tested to the system configuration | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Utilisation Stage | The stage in which the system is operated by users to deliver its intended services in the operational environment | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Support Stage | The stage in which the system is maintained and sustained to ensure continued availability and capability | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Retirement Stage | The stage in which the system is taken out of service; it is archived, decommissioned, or disposed of | ISO 15288 §6.2 |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. What is the primary purpose of the Concept Stage? What key artefact does it produce?
2. How does the Support Stage differ from the Utilisation Stage?
3. At which stage is V&V (verification and validation) first formally applied?
4. Why is the Retirement Stage often overlooked in planning — and what are the consequences?
5. In what stage would you find the System Verification Review (SVR)?

### Day 3 — Wednesday | Decision Gates — Stage Transition Reviews
**Estimated Time:** 30 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Chapter 2 — decision gates and stage transition reviews

#### Topics to Cover
- What a decision gate is and why it exists
- ISO 15288 terminology: "decision gate", "stage boundary", "life cycle decision review"
- Typical outputs of a decision gate review: proceed / conditional proceed / return / terminate
- Typical decision reviews: System Requirements Review (SRR), Preliminary Design Review (PDR), Critical Design Review (CDR), System Verification Review (SVR), Operational Readiness Review (ORR)
- The relationship between decision gate outcomes and risk management
- Who authorises stage transitions (acquirer/supplier roles)

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2 sections on decision gates and stage transition reviews
- [ ] Map the common review names (SRR, PDR, CDR, SVR, ORR) to their respective stage transitions
- [ ] Write a 3-sentence explanation of why a "terminate" outcome at a decision gate is valuable — not just a failure
- [ ] Review Day 3 Flashcards (Cards 21–28)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Decision Gate | A formal review point at the boundary between life cycle stages used to assess readiness and authorise (or deny) progression to the next stage | SEH5 Ch2; ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Stage Transition Review | The formal review conducted at a decision gate to determine whether exit criteria for the current stage have been met | ISO 15288 §6.2 |
| Exit Criteria | The set of conditions that must be satisfied before a system may progress to the next life cycle stage | SEH5 Ch2 |
| System Requirements Review (SRR) | A formal review verifying that system requirements have been adequately defined and are consistent with stakeholder needs — typically at the Concept/Development boundary | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Critical Design Review (CDR) | A formal review assessing whether the detailed design is ready to proceed to production — typically mid-Development stage | SEH5 Ch2 |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. What four outcomes can a decision gate review produce?
2. Where in the life cycle would you expect a Preliminary Design Review (PDR)?
3. What is the difference between entry criteria and exit criteria for a stage?
4. Who has authority to approve stage transitions — the acquirer, supplier, or both?

### Day 4 — Thursday | Life Cycle Models
**Estimated Time:** 35 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Chapter 2 — life cycle models and tailoring

#### Topics to Cover
- **Sequential (Waterfall-like) model** — linear progression, clear gates, suited to stable requirements
- **Iterative model** — repeated cycles of development, suited to evolving requirements
- **Concurrent model** — overlapping stages, accelerates development but raises risk
- **Evolutionary model** — multiple incremental capability deliveries
- **Agile-SE model** — iterative sprints within an overarching SE framework; how ISO 15288 processes apply within sprints
- When each model is appropriate — exam scenarios
- Life cycle model tailoring: why one size does not fit all

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2 sections on life cycle models
- [ ] For each model, note: (a) when it is most appropriate, (b) one risk, (c) one benefit
- [ ] Draw a simple timeline diagram of the sequential vs iterative models from memory
- [ ] Review Day 4 Flashcards (Cards 29–38)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Sequential Life Cycle Model | A life cycle model in which stages are executed in a prescribed order with each stage largely completed before the next begins | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Iterative Life Cycle Model | A life cycle model in which the system is progressively refined through multiple successive cycles, each producing a more complete or capable version | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Concurrent Life Cycle Model | A life cycle model in which multiple life cycle stages are conducted simultaneously to reduce schedule | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Evolutionary Life Cycle Model | A life cycle model in which the system is delivered in incremental capability releases, each building upon the last | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Life Cycle Model Tailoring | The adaptation of a standard life cycle model to fit the specific characteristics, risks, and constraints of a given programme | SEH5 Ch2 |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. Under what conditions would you select a sequential model over an iterative model?
2. What is the principal risk of a concurrent life cycle model?
3. How does an Agile-SE approach differ from a pure Agile software development approach?
4. What does "life cycle model tailoring" mean, and who performs it?

### Day 5 — Friday | Processes vs Stages — The Critical Distinction
**Estimated Time:** 35 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Chapter 2 — processes and stages; SEH5 Chapter 3 introduction

#### Topics to Cover
- ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 defines **processes** — not stages — as its primary construct
- The four ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 process groups: Technical, Technical Management, Agreement, and Organisational Project-Enabling
- A process can be applied in **any** stage — e.g., the Verification process operates in Development, Production, and Support
- Stages tell you **when**; processes tell you **what activities to perform**
- Common CSEP exam trap: confusing "the Development stage" with "the Development process"
- How a programme selects and applies processes within each stage (process application planning)

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2 sections on the process/stage distinction, and skim Chapter 3 introduction
- [ ] Build a 4×6 matrix (four process groups × six stages) and mark which process groups operate in each stage
- [ ] Write a paragraph explaining why the Verification process can run in both the Development and Production stages
- [ ] Review Day 5 Flashcards (Cards 39–46)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Life Cycle Process | A set of interrelated activities that transform inputs into outputs, performed in any applicable life cycle stage | ISO 15288 §6.1 |
| Technical Processes | The ISO 15288 process group concerned with the technical activities of engineering the system (requirements, design, implementation, V&V, etc.) | ISO 15288 §6.4 |
| Technical Management Processes | The ISO 15288 process group concerned with managing the technical work — planning, scheduling, risk management, configuration management, etc. | ISO 15288 §6.3 |
| Agreement Processes | The ISO 15288 process group governing acquirer–supplier relationships: Acquisition and Supply | ISO 15288 §6.1 |
| Organisational Project-Enabling Processes | The ISO 15288 process group concerned with the organisation's infrastructure and project resources: life cycle model management, infrastructure management, portfolio management, HRM, QAMS, KM | ISO 15288 §6.2 |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. Name the four ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 process groups.
2. Can the Verification process be applied in the Support stage? Justify your answer.
3. What is the key distinction between a "stage" and a "process" in ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288?
4. In which process group would Configuration Management be found?

### Day 6 — Saturday | Cost of Change and Life Cycle Thinking in Practice
**Estimated Time:** 30 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Chapter 2 — cost of change; SEH5 Chapter 1 (SE value proposition revisited)

#### Topics to Cover
- The cost-of-change curve: cost increases exponentially with progression through the life cycle
- Barry Boehm's research underpinning the curve — and its limits
- Why early SE investment (requirements, architecture, V&V planning) in the Concept and Development stages dramatically reduces total life cycle cost (TLC)
- Life cycle cost (LCC) vs acquisition cost — why LCC is the correct metric for SE decisions
- The role of SE in managing total ownership cost across all six stages
- Common SE failure modes attributable to poor life cycle thinking (late requirements changes, missing retirement planning, deferred V&V)

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2 sections on cost of change and life cycle thinking
- [ ] Sketch the cost-of-change curve from memory and annotate which stage each region corresponds to
- [ ] Write three SE decisions that, if made poorly in the Concept stage, create disproportionately large costs in later stages
- [ ] Review Day 6 Flashcards (Cards 47–55)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Life Cycle Cost (LCC) | The total cost of a system across all stages — concept through retirement — including acquisition, operation, support, and disposal | SEH5 Ch2 |
| Cost of Change | The relative cost of correcting an error or implementing a change, which increases exponentially as progression through the life cycle increases | SEH5 Ch2 (citing Boehm) |
| Total Ownership Cost | The complete cost of owning and operating a system over its life cycle, including all support and disposal costs — used interchangeably with LCC in many programmes | SEH5 Ch2 |
| SE Value Proposition | The argument that early SE investment (especially in requirements and architecture) reduces total life cycle cost by preventing costly late-stage changes | SEH5 Ch1 & Ch2 |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. Describe the cost-of-change curve and what it implies for when SE effort should be front-loaded.
2. Why is acquisition cost a misleading metric for comparing system options?
3. Give two examples where poor requirements definition in the Concept stage caused large costs in the Production or Utilisation stage.
4. What is the SE argument for investing heavily in the Concept stage even when there is schedule pressure to begin Development?
