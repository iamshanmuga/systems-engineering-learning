# CSEP Study Plan — Week 1: Systems Engineering Overview & Fundamentals
**Certification:** INCOSE CSEP (Certified Systems Engineering Professional)
**Primary Reference:** INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook, 5th Edition (SEH5)
**Exam Basis:** ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023 — System Life Cycle Processes
**Week Theme:** What Systems Engineering is, why it exists, and the foundational principles that underpin it
**Target:** ~4.5 hours total study time across 7 days

---

## 🎯 Week 1 Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 1, you should be able to:

1. Recite the INCOSE and ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 definitions of Systems Engineering from memory
2. Define and distinguish key SE vocabulary: system, system-of-interest, enabling system, system element, system boundary, and emergence
3. Explain why SE exists and what problems it solves that single-discipline engineering cannot
4. Describe the core characteristics of a system and distinguish open from closed systems
5. Articulate the key ideas behind systems thinking and contrast it with reductionist thinking
6. Name the key contributors to Systems Science (von Bertalanffy, Forrester, Meadows, Senge)
7. Apply all core SE principles: holism, abstraction, hierarchy, modularity, encapsulation, separation of concerns, feedback, and evolvability
8. Explain what interfaces are and why interface management is critical in SE
9. Name common SE project failure modes per INCOSE research
10. Identify the four ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 process groups at a high level (preview for Week 3 onward)
11. Describe the CSEP exam structure and scoring approach

**Estimated Time:** 30 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Part I, Chapter 1 — Systems Engineering Introduction (first half)

#### Topics to Cover
- The INCOSE definition of SE
- The ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 definition of SE
- Why SE exists — the "wicked problems" and complexity SE is designed to manage
- SE vs. traditional single-discipline engineering

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 1, first half (focus on SE definitions and motivation)
- [ ] After reading, write both SE definitions from memory without looking
- [ ] In your own words, write 3 sentences on "why SE exists"
- [ ] Review Day 1 Flashcards (Cards 1–10)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Systems Engineering (INCOSE) | "An interdisciplinary approach and means to enable the realization of successful systems. It focuses on defining customer needs and required functionality early in the development cycle, documenting requirements, then proceeding with design synthesis and system validation while considering the complete problem." |
| Systems Engineering (ISO 15288) | A set of processes, methods, and techniques for defining, realising, sustaining, and retiring a system-of-interest. |

#### Self-Check Questions

2. State the ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 definition of SE from memory.
3. Name three types of complexity that SE is designed to manage.
4. Why can traditional single-discipline engineering fail when applied to complex systems?

### Day 2 — Tuesday | Systems — Key Concepts & Vocabulary
**Estimated Time:** 30 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Part I, Chapter 1 — Systems Engineering Introduction (second half)

#### Topics to Cover
- What makes something a "system" — elements, relationships, boundary, environment, purpose
- System-of-Interest (SoI) — definition and examples
- Enabling systems — types, examples, and how they differ from the SoI
- System elements — the constituent parts of a system
- System boundary — what it is, who defines it, and why it matters
- Emergence — the defining property of systems
- Open vs. closed systems

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 1, second half (key concepts and vocabulary section)
- [ ] For a system of your choice, identify: its elements, boundary, environment, the SoI, and at least one enabling system
- [ ] Write your own definition of "emergence" and give two real-world examples
- [ ] Review Day 2 Flashcards (Cards 11–20)

#### Key Definitions to Memorise
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| System | A set of interacting or interdependent elements forming a unified whole with a defined boundary, operating in an environment to achieve defined objectives |
| System-of-Interest (SoI) | The specific system being analysed, designed, or developed in the context of a project or study |
| Enabling System | A system that provides services needed to support the SoI during one or more life cycle stages |
| System Element | Any constituent part of a system — hardware, software, data, processes, personnel, or facilities |
| Emergence | A system-level property arising from interactions among elements, not possessed by any single element alone |
| System Boundary | The conceptual or physical line separating the SoI from its external environment |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. What distinguishes a system from a mere collection of parts?
2. Give two examples of enabling systems for a commercial aircraft.
3. Why does defining the system boundary matter — and why might different stakeholders draw it differently?
4. Give a real-world example of an emergent property.

### Day 3 — Wednesday | Systems Thinking & Systems Science
**Estimated Time:** 45 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Part I, Chapter 2 — Systems Science and Systems Thinking

#### Topics to Cover
- Systems Science — origins and key contributors (von Bertalanffy, Forrester, Meadows, Senge)
- Systems thinking vs. reductionist (analytical) thinking — the critical contrast
- Feedback loops: negative (balancing) and positive (reinforcing), with examples
- System Dynamics (Forrester) — modelling feedback and time delays
- "Wicked problems" — definition and why they require a systems approach
- Complexity characteristics: non-linearity, feedback, emergence, self-organisation

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 2 in full
- [ ] Draw a simple causal loop diagram showing a negative feedback loop (e.g., thermostat) and a positive feedback loop (e.g., population growth)
- [ ] Write a 4-sentence contrast between systems thinking and reductionist thinking — without looking at your notes
- [ ] Review Day 3 Flashcards (Cards 21–32)

#### Key Contrast Table
| Reductionist Thinking | Systems Thinking |
|---|---|
| Break into parts; solve each separately | Understand the whole and its interactions |
| Assumes whole = sum of parts | Recognises emergence — whole ≠ sum of parts |
| Linear cause-and-effect | Non-linear feedback loops and time delays |
| Optimises individual components | Optimises system-level behaviour |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. Who founded General Systems Theory, and in what era?
2. What distinguishes systems thinking from reductionist thinking?
3. What is the difference between positive and negative feedback loops? Give an example of each.
4. What is a "wicked problem"? Name two of its characteristics.

### Day 4 — Thursday | SE Principles — Part 1
**Estimated Time:** 45 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Part I, Chapter 3 — SE Principles (first half)

#### Topics to Cover
- **Holism** — understanding and managing the whole, not just optimising the parts
- **Hierarchy** — systems within systems; nesting of SoS, system, subsystem, and element
- **Abstraction** — representing a system at the level of detail appropriate to the problem
- **Encapsulation** — hiding internal implementation behind a well-defined interface
- **Modularity** — designing loosely coupled, highly cohesive system elements

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 3, first half
- [ ] For each principle, write your own one-sentence definition and a concrete engineering example
- [ ] Sketch a hierarchy diagram: System-of-Systems → System → Subsystem → Element
- [ ] Review Day 4 Flashcards (Cards 33–40)

#### Principles Summary Table
| Principle | Core Idea | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Holism | The whole is greater than the sum of its parts | Aircraft performance ≠ sum of individual component specs |
| Hierarchy | Systems nest within systems at multiple levels | Army > Division > Battalion > Soldier |
| Abstraction | Hide detail to manage complexity | UML block diagram vs. detailed circuit schematic |
| Encapsulation | Internal detail hidden behind a defined interface | Black-box component: input/output defined, internals hidden |
| Modularity | Loosely coupled, highly cohesive units | Hot-swappable hardware modules |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. What is holism, and why does it matter in SE?
2. Why is abstraction important when designing complex systems?
3. What is the difference between encapsulation and modularity?
4. Sketch a simple hierarchy diagram for a naval combat system.

### Day 5 — Friday | SE Principles — Part 2 & Interfaces
**Estimated Time:** 45 minutes
**SEH5 Coverage:** Part I, Chapter 3 — SE Principles (second half)

#### Topics to Cover
- **Separation of Concerns** — addressing different aspects of a system independently
- **Feedback** — using system outputs to influence inputs; control and adaptation
- **Evolvability** — designing a system to accommodate future change without redesign
- **Interfaces** — the shared boundary across which signals, data, energy, or material pass
- Why most system failures occur at interfaces
- Open vs. closed systems revisited through the lens of interfaces and environment interaction

#### Study Activities
- [ ] Read SEH5 Chapter 3, second half
- [ ] For a system you know well (e.g., a car, smartphone), identify three interfaces and describe what crosses each boundary
- [ ] Write two sentences explaining why interfaces are where most SE failures occur
- [ ] Review Day 5 Flashcards (Cards 41–50)

#### Interface Concepts
| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| Interface | The shared boundary between two system elements across which signals, data, energy, material, or humans pass |
| Interface failure risk | Most system integration failures originate at poorly defined or uncontrolled interfaces |
| Interface Control Document (ICD) | Formal specification of an interface — covered in depth from Week 3 onward |

#### Self-Check Questions
1. What is separation of concerns and how does it simplify complex system design?
2. Give an example of feedback in a human-machine system.
3. What is evolvability, and why should it be designed in from the start of a project?
4. Why do most system integration failures occur at interfaces rather than within elements?
